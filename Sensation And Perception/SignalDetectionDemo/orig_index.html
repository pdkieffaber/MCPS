Airplane Signal Detection Demo

Signal Detection Theory

Signal detection theory explains how people make decisions under uncertainty. In many real-world settings, the evidence is noisy or ambiguous, so observers must decide whether a signal is present even when they are not fully sure.

Radar Example

A radar operator might need to decide whether one object on the screen indicates a meaningful target or whether what they see is just background noise. In this classroom demonstration, the moving airplanes stand in for potential radar targets. On some trials, one airplane moves faster than the others. On other trials, all airplanes move at the same speed.

Possible Outcomes

There are four basic outcomes in signal detection theory:

Hit: the signal is present and the observer says “signal present.”
Miss: the signal is present and the observer says “no signal.”
False alarm: no signal is present, but the observer says “signal present.”
Correct rejection: no signal is present and the observer says “no signal.”

Sensitivity and Response Bias

Sensitivity refers to how well a person can distinguish signal trials from no-signal trials. Sensitivity goes up when the signal becomes easier to detect.

Response bias refers to a person’s tendency to favor one response over another. For example, if missing a target is costly, a person may become more likely to say “signal present,” even when uncertain.

What Changes Across Blocks

In this demonstration, the first block serves as a baseline. In the second block, the target is easier to discriminate, which should increase sensitivity. In the third block, the instructions encourage a more liberal response tendency, which should shift response bias.
